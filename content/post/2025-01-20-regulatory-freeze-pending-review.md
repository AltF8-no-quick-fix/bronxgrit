**I. Detailed Analysis of the Presidential Action: "Regulatory Freeze Pending Review"**

This presidential memorandum is a standard practice for incoming administrations.  It's a tool to immediately halt or delay the regulatory activity of the previous administration, allowing the new administration to review and potentially reshape, rescind, or implement those regulations according to its own policy priorities.  Here's a breakdown:

*   **Purpose:** To pause the regulatory process and allow the new administration to review, approve, or reject regulations initiated by the previous administration. This ensures that regulations align with the new President's policy agenda.

*   **Scope:**  Extremely broad. It covers not just formal "rules" (as defined by the Administrative Procedure Act), but also *any* "regulatory action" and even "guidance documents." This includes notices of inquiry, proposed rulemakings – essentially anything that *could* lead to a regulation.  This breadth is significant because it gives the new administration maximum control over the regulatory landscape.

*   **Mechanism:**
    *   **Immediate Halt:**  No new rules can be proposed or issued without approval from a department/agency head appointed by the *new* President.
    *   **Withdrawal:**  Rules sent to the Federal Register but not yet published are withdrawn.
    *   **Postponement:**  Rules already published but not yet effective are postponed for 60 days, with the possibility of further delays.  This provides a window for review and potential modification.
    *   **Public Comment:**  The memorandum encourages opening a comment period for postponed rules, allowing stakeholders to weigh in.  This *could* be used to build a case for altering or rescinding a rule.
    *   **OMB Director's Role:**  The Office of Management and Budget (OMB) Director is given significant power. They oversee the entire process, can grant exemptions, and consult with agencies on further actions for rules that raise "substantial questions." This centralizes control over the regulatory freeze within the White House.

*   **Legal Basis:**  The memorandum cites the President's authority under the Constitution and laws of the United States.  This is a general claim of executive power, and the specific legal basis for each action would depend on the particular regulation being affected. The Administrative Procedure Act (APA) provides the framework for rulemaking, and any changes or delays must comply with the APA's requirements.

*   **Exceptions:**  The OMB Director can exempt rules deemed necessary for emergencies or to meet statutory/judicial deadlines. This provides flexibility to address urgent situations.

*   **Policy Shift:** The most significant long-term impact is the potential for a substantial shift in regulatory policy.  The new administration can use this freeze to:
    *   **Block Regulations:**  Prevent regulations it opposes from taking effect.
    *   **Weaken Regulations:**  Modify regulations to make them less stringent or burdensome.
    *   **Delay Implementation:**  Postpone regulations indefinitely, effectively killing them through inaction.
    *   **Promote New Regulations:**  Clear the way for its own regulatory agenda.

*   **Uncertainty for Businesses and Stakeholders:**  Regulatory freezes create uncertainty. Businesses, state governments, and other stakeholders may be unsure about which rules will ultimately be in effect, making planning and investment decisions difficult.

## Beyond the Headlines: A Critical Look

This presidential memorandum, ordering a regulatory freeze, presents several significant legal and practical challenges.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Intent:** The memorandum clearly articulates the intention to review all pending and proposed regulations.
* **Delegation of Authority:** The delegation of review power to appointed officials is a practical approach to handling the volume of regulations.
* **Exemptions for Emergencies:**  The inclusion of exceptions for emergency situations is necessary to prevent paralysis in critical areas.
* **Review of Existing Rules:** The directive to withdraw rules not yet published and to postpone effective dates for published rules acknowledges that existing regulations might need review.
* **Defined Scope:** The definition of "rule" encompasses a broad range of agency actions, ensuring a comprehensive review.
* **Centralized Oversight:** Establishing OMB Director oversight for implementation provides a clear point of contact and coordination.

**Weaknesses and Potential Legal Challenges:**

* **Constitutionality Concerns:**  The breadth of the freeze, particularly its application to all proposed regulations, raises potential separation of powers concerns.  The President's authority to unilaterally halt the normal operations of executive agencies may be challenged by the judiciary.  The memorandum needs to carefully address the relevant statutory authorities governing agency rulemaking.  The potential impact on ongoing legal proceedings and established processes for rulemaking need to be carefully weighed.
* **Due Process Concerns:** Postponing effective dates and opening comment periods for already published rules without clear legal justification could raise due process issues for those affected by the rules.  The memorandum needs to address this directly by providing a reasoned explanation for the delay.
* **Practical Implementation:**  The memorandum's complexity and broad scope are likely to lead to significant administrative burden and confusion within the executive branch. The process for reviewing and approving regulations, especially considering the time constraints, needs to be meticulously designed to ensure efficiency and prevent stagnation.
* **Disruption of Policy Implementation:** The freeze could disrupt ongoing policy implementation, potentially creating uncertainty and instability

## The Source Material: Executive Order Text

Presidential Actions				
			
							
					Regulatory Freeze Pending Review 				
			
			
				
										
					
						January 20, 2025					
				

			
					
	




By the authority vested in me as President by the Constitution and the laws of the United States of America, I hereby order all executive departments and agencies to take the following steps:



(1)  Do not propose or issue any rule in any manner, including by sending a rule to the Office of the Federal Register (the “OFR”), until a department or agency head appointed or designated by the President after noon on January 20, 2025, reviews and approves the rule.  The department or agency head may delegate this power of review and approval to any other person so appointed or designated by the President, consistent with applicable law.  The Director or Acting Director of the Office of Management and Budget (the “OMB Director”) may exempt any rule that he deems necessary to address emergency situations or other urgent circumstances, including rules subject to statutory or judicial deadlines that require prompt action.



(2)  Immediately withdraw any rules that have been sent to the OFR but not published in the Federal Register, so that they can be reviewed and approved as described in paragraph 1, subject to the exceptions described in paragraph 1. 



(3)  Consistent with applicable law and subject to the exceptions described in paragraph 1, consider postponing for 60 days from the date of this memorandum the effective date for any rules that have been published in the Federal Register, or any rules that have been issued in any manner but have not taken effect, for the purpose of reviewing any questions of fact, law, and policy that the rules may raise.  During this 60-day period, where appropriate and consistent with applicable law, consider opening a comment period to allow interested parties to provide comments about issues of fact, law, and policy raised by the rules postponed under this memorandum, and consider reevaluating pending petitions involving such rules.  As appropriate and consistent with applicable law, and where necessary to continue to review these questions of fact, law, and policy, consider further delaying, or publishing for notice and comment, proposed rules further delaying such rules beyond the 60-day period.



(4)  Following the postponement described in paragraph 3, no further action needs to be taken for those rules that raise no substantial questions of fact, law, or policy.  For those rules that raise substantial questions of fact, law, or policy, agencies should notify and take further appropriate action in consultation with the OMB Director.



(5)  Comply in all circumstances with any applicable Executive Orders concerning regulatory management.



As used in this memorandum, “rule” has the definition set forth in section 551(4), title 5, United States Code.  It also includes any “regulatory action,” as defined in section 3(e) of Executive Order 12866 of September 30, 1993, as amended, and any “guidance document” as defined in section 2(b) of Executive Order 13891 of October 9, 2019 (Promoting the Rule of Law Through Improved Agency Guidance Documents), when that order was in effect.  Thus, the requirements of this memorandum apply not only to “rules” as defined in section 551(4) of title 5, but also to any substantive action by an agency (normally published in the Federal Register) that promulgates or is expected to lead to the promulgation of a final rule or regulation, including notices of inquiry, advance notices of proposed rulemaking, and notices of proposed rulemaking.  They shall also apply to any agency statement of general applicability and future effect that sets forth a policy on a statutory, regulatory, or technical issue or an interpretation of a statutory or regulatory issue.



The OMB Director shall oversee the implementation of this memorandum, and any communications regarding any matters pertaining to this review should be addressed to the OMB Director.  The OMB Director is also authorized to establish a process to review pending collections of information under the Paperwork Reduction Act of 1995, as codified in chapter 35, title 44, United States Code, and to take actions that the OMB Director deems appropriate based on that review, consistent with applicable law.



Should actions be identified that were undertaken before noon on January 20, 2025, that frustrate the purpose underlying this memorandum, I may modify or extend this memorandum, to require that department and agency heads consider taking steps to address those actions.



The OMB Director is authorized and directed to publish this memorandum in the Federal Register.



This memorandum shall be implemented consistent with applicable law.
